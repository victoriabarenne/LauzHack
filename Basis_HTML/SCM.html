Model 1 : Mixed-densisty model

For this model, we thought it appropriate to divide our interactions into two levels, differentiating local and global ones. Indeed, if we take a look at any individual in our society, we will always notice a more specific group with which interactions are more frequent, be it family, coworkers or just a local community.

This is an important aspect to take into account when modeling an epidemic. Once the virus reaches a local community, the rate of the spread within this community becomes significantly higher than at a global level.

Take the example of a family: once a family member is infected, there’s a very high likelihood the rest of the family gets infected, much higher than the chance of them getting infected by an outsider.

This model produces a basic reproductive rate R0, i.e. the average number of people a single infected individual will pass the virus onto. The following two models take this parameter into account

Model 2 : SIS Model

The first model we used to represent an epidemic, is an SIS model, where the considered population is divided into two categories:
S, the healthy individuals, susceptible of contracting the virus,
I, the infected individuals.

The model works as follows:
Each infected individual comes in contact with β healthy individuals, which will then contract the virus
The rate of recovery is γ: once an individual has recovered, he is considered healthy again and is not exempt from contracting the virus a second time
We consider a birth rate and death rate equal to b, allowing us to keep the considered population steady
