In this section, we provide a few one-on-one simulation comparisons as an example of what you can do with our comparison mode tab and some interesting conclusions one can draw from them.

These simulations were done with a total population of a million, divided into smaller communities of 100 individuals (meaning there are 10,000 communities).
And the parameters we changed during these simulations were:

  • μG, the average global infections caused by a single infected individual,
  • μL, the level of local confinement, i.e. the exposure of indiviuals to other within their local community

    1: low level of confinement,
    2: moderate level of confinement,
    3: high level of confinement.

  • D, the average duration of the infection, during which individuals are contagious.

A smaller μG (resp. μL) represents less frequent interactions on a global (resp. local) level. Thus, changing these parameters will let you represent different degrees of confinement or behavior during confinement.

Note to reader: in the following comparaisons, we used the SIR continuous model described here.

1. Low vs. High level of Global Confinement

Left: μG=0.25, moderate local confinement, D=7 and Right: μG=2, moderate:50, D=7

In this comparaison, we only change the level of the global confinement. With a more lax confinement policy, as in the second graph, the equilibrium stage is reached sooner. This explains the argument in favor of collective immunity adopted by many countries in the present case of COVID-19 disease: the sooner the equilibrium is reached, the faster we can restart the economy and get back to our usual routines.

While at first glance, this might seem like the fastest way to end the pandemic, the reality is much more nuanced. We see that in the low local confinement model, the peak of infected people goes up to 800,000, versus 600,000 in the high local confinement model. With a higher peak, there are more chances of the healthcare system being overworked, resulting in a higher total mortality rate.

2. 7 vs. 20 days of Virus Duration

Left: μG=0.75, high local confinement, D=7 and Right: μG=0.75, high local confinement, D=20

Here, having fixed all other parameters, we can observe the impact the duration of infection has on the spread. In both cases, the maximum number of infected individuals at a time are equal. However, with a 3 weeks duration of infection, the epidemic lasts 3 times as long.

3. Mixed Comparison

Left: μG=2, high local confinement, D=7 and Right: μG=0.25, low local confinement, D=7

We compared these models to see whether we should privilege local or global confinement. Both models reach the peak of the epidemic quite fast. A noticeable difference is the value of the peak, which is lower in high global confinement/low local confinement than in low global confinement/high local confinement. This shows that global confinement has a higher impact on slowing down the spread of the virus than local confinement.

These results make sense. With really few global interactions, once the virus hits a local community, worse case scenario all 100 individuals of the community get infected; with very little chances of spreading to other communities. Whereas with a lot of global interactions, it's very likely a high proportion of the 10000 communities get an infected individual. Even if, locally, the virus doesn't spread, you've already infected way more than a few hundreds of individuals.

Of course these results are due to our choice of global and local communities. We leave it to the reader to decide whether this model would be valid in representing a big city and its smaller inner-city communities.
